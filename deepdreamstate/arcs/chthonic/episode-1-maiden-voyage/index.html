Ding, Dong, Dagon.

The Chthonic sets sail, promising luxury, immersion, and a good time. It'll keep one of those promises.

Cruise Director Olivia runs the welcome with practiced warmth. The captain stays mostly out of sight, and the app knows where everyone is.

The bell rings every day to declare "everything is fine!"

Below decks, the crew knows better. Spa manager Mairead is broke and increasingly desperate. Engineer Fion watches and says nothing useful. Purser Selene has worked ships like this before, and she is not surprised by anything she sees. When Nikki comes back from Room 203, nobody in management wants to hear about it.

Up top, a bachelorette party is having the time of many lives. They know something's amiss, though. They can't drink away the strange sounds through the walls, or the much-too-specific dreams. The ship seems to be noticing them back, and that no longer feels like a dream anyone can write off.

Emma feels the uncanny, but her friends are already ordering another round.

By the end of the first night, someone has been identified. The machinery has a target, and the voyage has its first real purpose.

Arc 1: Chthonic

A luxury cruise wedding party sails into an ancient ritual. The ceremony was beneath the ice. Now it's free. Every celebration aboard conceals something older and wetter. By the end of the voyage, there might not be a way out.

Chthonic begins with a ship full of passengers expecting curated pleasure. They discover more than a hidden cult beneath the waves and the ice. The parties, the ceremonies, the games, the flirtations, the humiliations people try to laugh away. All of it fits together.

Each episode strips away another layer of ordinary social performance. Guests who boarded the ship trying to manage appearances slowly find themselves participating in something older than embarrassment. Some resist and some lean in. Most drift toward it piece by piece until the distinction between terror and desire stops feeling stable.

By Spiralstorm, the voyage transformed into liturgy. The passengers stop asking if the ritual is real and start asking if they were ever living outside it in the first place.
