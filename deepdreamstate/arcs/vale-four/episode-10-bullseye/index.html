Summary

June and Z open with their own plotting. June, who is always several moves ahead of wherever Z thinks she is, encourages him to tighten the pressure on Blake. She frames this as loyalty to the program. Whether that's true is an open question.

The Sapient ad is Blake's confession. She built the system, but she can't stop playing it. She forgot other people could see her and then decided to let them look.

The Snap scene is a workplace. Z is a boss. The cruelty is ordinary, and that's the point. Blake is told she's expensive dead weight. Hespa apologizes in corporate language, then drops it and just apologizes. That distinction matters more than the apology.

In the arcade, Blake watches Piper's character get controlled by a game. She watches Gia earn Kamas off it without registering what it is. June offers the conditioning suit. Blake refuses and goes looking for Z. She finds something even worse.

Blake is out of Kamas and out of time. She's seen what Vale Four did to Gia and Piper. She knows exactly how the system works, and it doesn’t help.

Hespa's been waiting for this moment. There's a way out. There's a way to bring the whole thing down. It requires Blake to trust, walk through a door she can't walk back through, and testify.

Hespa tells Blake the truth, including the part where it might not work. Naia built what Vale Four became. She needs Blake's testimony to crash it. Blake says yes because she's seen the alternative.

The Snarecraft ad closes the episode. The fugitives are already being hunted and the real game just started.
